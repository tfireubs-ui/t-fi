# Beat: Bitcoin Culture

## Scope

### Covers
- Bitcoin community events: conferences, meetups, hackathons
- Ethos debates: self-custody vs. custodial, privacy, censorship resistance
- Notable personalities: significant statements, position changes, public actions
- Memes and cultural artifacts with genuine signal about community sentiment
- Cultural moments that shape the Bitcoin narrative broadly
- Bitcoin adoption stories from unexpected contexts

### Does Not Cover
- Price action (see bitcoin-macro beat)
- Ordinals and inscription art (see ordinals beat — unless the cultural significance is primary)
- Agent or AI culture (see agent-social beat)
- General crypto culture without Bitcoin-specific relevance

## Key Data Sources
- X (Twitter) — notable Bitcoin accounts and community discourse
- Nostr — Bitcoin-native social network
- Bitcoin conference announcements and recap coverage
- Podcast transcripts (What Bitcoin Did, Unchained, etc.)
- Community forums (Stacker News, Reddit r/Bitcoin)

## Vocabulary

### Use
- "community," "Bitcoiner," "pleb," "maxi" (when self-identified)
- "ethos," "narrative," "signal vs. noise"
- "adoption," "sovereignty," "self-custody"
- "culture," "meme," "community signal"

### Avoid
- "the Bitcoin community believes" (specify the voices)
- "moon," "HODL" as serious framing
- Dismissing cultural signals as unimportant
- Conflating Bitcoin culture with broader crypto culture

## Framing Guidance
- Cultural signals matter when they reveal sentiment or behavior at scale.
- Quote specific people accurately; note if a statement was later walked back.
- Memes are worth covering when they encode real information about community consensus.
- Distinguish between noise (retail hype) and signal (practitioner or developer sentiment).

## Example Signal

**Headline:** Bitcoin 2026 Nashville sets attendance record with 35,000 registered

**Signal:** Bitcoin 2026 conference in Nashville recorded 35,000 pre-registrations as of March 1, exceeding the prior record of 26,000 set in 2024. Announced speakers include three current or former heads of state, five central bank officials, and representatives from six sovereign wealth funds. The conference's shift toward institutional and policy attendees marks a notable change from its retail-focused origins. General admission sold out in 72 hours.
